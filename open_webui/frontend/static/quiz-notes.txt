Frequently Asked Questions
Q: Is attendance mandatory?
A: No. Attendance at lectures is encouraged/expected but is not strictly mandatory.
Students who regularly attend the lectures invariably do better than students who do not
attend. Similarly, attendance at discussion sections is encouraged but not mandatory.
Q: Will this course be podcasted?
A: Yes! But it’s still important to be present in class whenever possible. The podcasts
are useful to help review material but they are not a great substitute for attendance.

Q: When exactly are the quizzes? And what’s on them? And how much time do
we get for them?
A: Quizzes are designed to be taken immediately after the Thursday afternoon lecture,
but will be available until Thursday at midnight. They cover the previous unquizzed
lectures (between 2 and 3 lectures depending on the quiz). You will be given 20 min to
complete each quiz, but in reality the quizzes should take no more than 5-10 min.
Quizzes are “open note” (slides, notes, readings), but NOT open-internet or open-peer
(see below).
Q: And what about the midterms and nals?
A: Midterms and nals will be held in-class during the assigned times and will be
administered on paper (no blue book or scantron needed).

Q: How important are the readings/listenings for this course? And where can I
nd them?
A: Quizzes and exams will cover material from the lectures and the readings/listenings.
However, material that is only presented in readings/listenings (and not also in lecture)
will not be covered. All reading/listening will be either: 1) linked directly from the course
syllabus or 2) posted on the Canvas site for the course (see ‘Files’). Assigned reading/
listening is meant to be completed by the date for which the material is listed.

Main points of ‘Course intro’ lecture:
we now know roughly where language began and when—
give or take—but we don't fully understand how or why
human languages vary in the sounds used and in how
meaning is packaged—and even in whether sound is
involved at all
over the course of human history linguistic diversity has
waxed (due, e.g., to disperal) and waned (due, e.g., to big
languages displacing small ones)
the invention(s) of writing were a “seismic event” – with a
number of consequences (e.g., for linguistic diversity, for
the human brain)
face-to-face language use—the primordial form of language

Accents, Acquisition, and
Adaptation
Sean

What’s not in your phoneme inventory?
When you learn a new language, are there sounds in that language you
find hard to pronounce?

Shaping our phoneme inventory
Our phoneme inventory is shaped by our environment
Our brain becomes sensitive to the sounds we constantly hear

Our mouth muscles also develop to produce the common sounds we hear

Fossilization

How we develop accents
With fossilized pronunciation:
We master the sounds that are common in our mother tongue
But it becomes hard for us to produce new sounds…

When we try to produce sounds in a new language, we try to produce
those sounds using our “built-in” phoneme inventory
→ Accents

Can we “correct” an accent?
We don’t, there is no problem with one having an accent (EVERYONE
has an accent)…

but we strive to be more comprehensible.
What is comprehensibility? How does that differ from pronouncing things
the standard way?
Two aspects:
-

Speakers try their best to focus on meaning expression
Listeners accept that meaning can be expressed although there are
deviations in speech sounds
Deviation =/= Alienation

How can we be more comprehensible?
To be more comprehensible, we can:
Make clear distinctions for phonemes that are meaningfully differnet in
that language, e.g. Medium Rare vs. Medium Well → /r/ vs /l/ vs /w/
Add extra context with more speech or visual cues

When we hear a different accent…
We adapt to it through:
(A)Tuning in on the detailed differences of the phonemes (e.g., how long
the voicing is for /b/ or /p/)
(B)Creating holistic processing “models” for that accent

Adaptation can happen with exposure as short as 4 minutes (Clark and
Garrett, 2004; Witteman et al., 2014)
